NASA tv

Shifting the strategy and design of the NASA TV streaming service — modernising the viewing experience for a global, digital-first audience.

Role
End-to-end product design
Year
2026
Type
Self-initiated brief
Platform
Web · TV

The following is a case study of a self-imposed product design project demonstrating my design process from a project brief to production-ready design.

01 — Summary Project summary

Brief

Redesign NASA TV. NASA TV is the primary broadcast and digital media platform for delivering live missions, educational programming, and public-engagement content.

Goal

Modernise the viewing experience, improve accessibility, and better showcase NASA’s work for a global, digital-first audience.

Problem statement

How can NASA position its live TV offering to engage an ever-saturated digital content market?

My approach

My approach to product design resembles the widely established “double-diamond” method — progressively working through research, low- and high-fidelity design, prototyping, and hand-off.

Communication is critical at every step. Ideally I’d be checking in with users to validate at the milestones of low- and high-fidelity design, and with team members in front-end, back-end and data engineering to ensure feasibility.

02 — Discover UX research

Observations on the current service

My first instinct was to break down the current version of NASA TV. Some observations became clear quickly: the existing version doesn’t hold up to any modern visual UI standard, and it carries some glaring usability issues.

It’s difficult to see what content is currently playing live; viewing the wider schedule actually requires you to download a static .pdf file (which is only published in one timezone). Content is only available to be viewed live — there’s no on-demand element. There are also two streams running in parallel, with no clear difference between them.

Market research

I had a hypothesis going into research that this service wasn’t widely known about or really marketed by NASA. To check that, I turned to Google Trends to get some quantifiable data on whether people are searching for NASA TV specifically.

It looks like NASA TV may have drawn more of an audience when it first launched, but compared to the volume of users searching for NASA in general the numbers are relatively low. A strategy would have to be devised from a marketing standpoint to coincide with the launch of a new version — discoverability is an issue.

Research also led me to NASA’s YouTube page, where there’s clearly an appetite for the content NASA produces, but the live offering is lacking the same level of engagement. Being able to access on-demand content is a distinct difference from the NASA TV platform — and clearly something users are taking advantage of. This is the innate problem with a live-only service with no catch-up element.

Interestingly, NASA also runs their live channels through their YouTube page, but again only captures a small fraction of the views. With no exclusive content on the NASA TV platform, there’s really no compelling reason for people to visit NASA TV in its current form.

Looking beyond NASA's offerings I also found a few interesting examples of live streaming platforms that have successfully integrated live andon-demand content, which could serve as inspiration for the new NASA TV experience.

Part of the experience I am looking to uplift is also the visual UI design standard/aethetics, so I made time to gather design inspiration from other sources, not neccessarily within the video or streaming space.

03 — Define UX design

High level synthesis

After synthesising the research findings, I identified three key insights that would guide the design direction.

1. Quality programming wrapped in poor design.

The programming and live tv aspect of the product can and arguably has demonstrated it can provide significant value/entertainment for users.

The current visual design and user experience lacks quality and when combined with lack-luster marketing means NASA struggle to keep users engaged in a regular cadence on the NASA TV web app.

NASA have the ability to define the user experience on their own service unlike their YouTube channel.

2. Lack of an on-demand component.

Looking at other live platforms, it is clear users are interested in an on-demand component that can coexist with the live tv service. Why not emulate the success of their own Youtube channel? This can drive traffic and keep users engaged for longer.

There is an opportunity to learn from the design experience of the world’s most popular streaming services.

3. Does not offer anything that is exclusive to the platform.

The web app currently falls short at even emulating their YouTube offering. There is no compelling reason for users to visit the web application. Both in terms of content and user experience.

Design Objectives

This lead me to two distinct objectives to act as a north star for the design direction.

1. A new focus on delivering on-demand content.

Utilise a proven successful model by giving users the ability to access on-demand content a design an experience which facilitates this.

Make on-demand content an integral part of the experience without compromising or diminishing the value of live programming.

We must define a new user navigation experience. We do not want to stand in the way of the user to access live programming but also want to be clear on-demand is also the forefront of this product.

Less of a demand for scheduling if on-demand has more of a presence.

2. Not just a redesign but a relaunch.

A focus-shift to an alternative product strategy with the incorporation of on-demand programming now forming a major part of the offering.

A new brand that garners it’s own audience but is supported by the NASA parent brand.

If we are defining a new product can we develop it’s own visual language and extended brand identity. Purposeful separation for a new direction.

04 — Develop UI design

Defining visual style and brand

Taking my research findings into account, I began to define the visual style and brand identity for the new NASA TV sub-brand. My solution involved the more tradiotional NASA wordmark along side a red circled TV that represents live/on-air recordings.
